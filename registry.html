In Development Conceptual / Theoretical SES framework (not a computational model)
Key Question
What is a future forest as a social-ecological system — how do we define it, measure it, and talk about it across disciplines?
The theoretical and conceptual backbone of the entire Future Forests cluster. C1 develops a shared interdisciplinary social-ecological systems (SES) framework — including consistent definitions, indicators, accounting frameworks, and system boundaries — that all other research areas build on. It bridges natural science and social science vocabularies so that biophysical models and social models can be meaningfully connected.

Also: Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Dr. Florian Schnabel
In Development Machine learning / Spatial-temporal downscaling
Key Question
What are the exact temperature and humidity conditions inside and below forest canopies across the region?
Three-step approach: (1) quality control and adaptive network design using station uniqueness rankings; (2) ML hindcasting of in-canopy microclimates over 30 years; (3) transferable regional model integrating geospatial predictors, sensor data and physical downscaling to predict below-canopy temperatures at any forested location in the FuFo domain.

Prof. Dr. Thomas Seifert iLAND — Individual-based Forest Landscape and Disturbance Model
Also: Dr. Axel Albrecht · Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel · Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Jun.-Prof. Dr. Ana Stritih
Operational Process-based / Individual-based model
Key Question
How will individual trees and whole forest landscapes grow, die, and recover under different climates and management strategies?
Central biophysical engine of C3. Distance-dependent, individual tree-based forest growth simulator based on ecophysiological processes. Open-source, widely applied. Simulates forest dynamics from individual tree to small catchment including disturbances (wind, bark beetle, fire), regeneration, and silvicultural treatments. Includes ABE (Agent-Based Engine) module for management — direct interface to Stritih participatory modelling. Ref: Rammer et al. (2024), Ecol. Modelling 495, 110785.

Layer 2 — Biophysical Engine
Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler RoGeR — Runoff Generation Research Model
Operational Process-based hydrological model
Key Question
How does water move through soils and landscapes, and which trees are most vulnerable to drought because of where they stand?
Process-based hydrological model of water redistribution at the surface and subsurface, mediating local transpiration and trees vulnerability to drought. Three topographic contexts: plateau (1D, no lateral flow), hillslope (lateral subsurface in- and outflow), and riparian/valley (groundwater and capillary rise). Explicitly represents lateral subsurface flow and groundwater exchange — key processes missing from most dynamic vegetation models.
Inputs
Climate forcing: precipitation (Prec), potential evapotranspiration (PET)NetCDF / CSV← jung_schindler, christen
Soil properties: texture, depth, hydraulic conductivityGeoTIFF / CSV← weiler_gis
Topography: slope, aspect, catchment delineationGeoTIFF← weiler_gis
Canopy structure, leaf area index (LAI), root water uptake — bidirectional couplingNetCDF / CSV← iland
Outputs
Soil water content (SWC) in root zone, lateral subsurface flowNetCDF / CSV→ iland
Streamflow and catchment water balanceCSV→ brown, hanewinkel
Groundwater and capillary rise fluxesCSV / NetCDF→ iland

How uncertain are our model predictions, which uncertainties matter most, and how do we make models and data work together rigorously?
