In Development Scenario development / Statistical downscaling / Stakeholder co-design
This module produces the scenario infrastructure that all C3 models run on. It translates global frameworks (SSPs, RCPs, Nature's Futures Framework) into high-resolution, locally relevant inputs for the FuFo study regions. On the climate side, the focus is on downscaled projections with explicit treatment of extreme events and compound droughts. On the socio-economic side, narratives and quantitative pathways are co-created with stakeholders and partner institutions. The resulting products feed all C3 modelling groups and inform governance experimentation in C4.

Dr. Christopher Jung / Prof. Dr. Dirk Schindler HyClimScenarios
Also: Prof. Dr. Kerstin Stahl · Prof. Dr. Dirk Schindler · Prof. Dr. Andreas Christen · Prof. Dr. René Orth
In Development Statistical downscaling / Machine learning (tree-ensemble)
This project produces the high-resolution climate forcing that C3 models require, downscaling ERA5-Land reanalysis and CMIP6 projections from the ~10–120 km scale to a 100 m × 100 m grid across the study regions. Tree-ensemble machine learning is used for bias correction and statistical downscaling, covering historical conditions and future warming levels. Beyond standard climate variables, the project develops novel approaches for forest-relevant aspects of the water balance, including extreme events and event-storyline scenarios developed in collaboration with Stahl.

Prof. Dr. Kerstin Stahl Extreme Event Storylines & Coupled Groundwater Hydrology
Also: Prof. Dr. Dirk Schindler · Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler
In Development Extreme event storylines / Coupled surface-groundwater modelling
Stahl's contribution to C3 focuses on two connected areas. First, the development of extreme event storylines and stress-test scenarios — asking, for example, what would happen to forest SESs if the 2018–2022 drought sequence recurred under mid-century climate conditions. These scenarios go beyond standard ensemble projections to represent specific, high-impact events relevant for forest management. Second, the coupled surface-groundwater hydrological modelling, extending RoGeR with the groundwater model MODFLOW to assess how groundwater disconnection from forest root systems intensifies drought stress. Stahl co-leads C2 with Hanewinkel and is connected to six C3 groups in the workshop collaboration matrix.

Layer 1 — Observations & Climate Forcing
Adaptive Sensor Network & In-canopy Microclimate Model (A1)
Also: Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Dr. Florian Schnabel
In Development Machine learning / Spatial-temporal downscaling
Within-forest microclimates differ substantially from above-canopy conditions and are rarely modelled at scale. This project combines an adaptive sensor network with machine learning to reconstruct and predict fine-scale hydro-climatic conditions across the study regions. The network covers approximately 300 plots within 100–150 stands and is self-optimising — sites providing redundant information are progressively replaced. From this base, three modelling steps follow: quality-controlled station data are used to hindcast in-canopy conditions over 30 years, and the resulting model is extended into a transferable regional tool predicting below-canopy temperatures and humidity at any forested site in the domain.
Inputs
ERA5-Land reanalysis and HyClimScenarios climate projectionsNetCDF← Jung/Schindler (HyClimScenarios) / ECMWF
Sensor network time series — air temperature and humidity from ~300 field plotsCSV / HDF5← FuFo adaptive sensor network (A1)
Geospatial predictors: forest structure, terrain (slope, aspect, elevation), soil propertiesGeoTIFF / LAS← Weiler (GIS) / Seifert
Outputs
100 m x 100 m gridded in-canopy microclimate: air temperature, soil temperature, near-surface temperature; 30-year hindcast and climate extreme statisticsNetCDF / GeoTIFF→ iLAND, RoGeR, Stritih

Prof. Dr. Thomas Seifert LiDAR Forest Inventory and Dendroecology
Also: Dr. Axel Albrecht · Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel · Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler
In Development Remote sensing (LiDAR) / Dendroecology
This group provides the structural forest data needed to initialise iLAND and validate its simulations. Terrestrial (TLS) and UAV-based (ULS) laser scanning characterise individual tree positions, dimensions, and species across the study stands. Dendrochronological records add decades of annual growth response, giving iLAND both a realistic starting state and a long-term observational reference for calibration. The group also develops digital twin reconstructions of forest stands for structural analysis.

Prof. Dr. René Orth Forest Drought Response Functions
Also: Prof. Dr. Teja Kattenborn · Dr. Pia Müller
Operational Machine learning / Data synthesis
This group quantifies how forest function declines as water availability decreases, using global satellite observations, eddy covariance flux tower data, and plant traits from the TRY database. The resulting species-specific response functions — relating photosynthesis and transpiration to soil water content and vapour pressure deficit — are embedded into iLAND to constrain its drought mortality representation. Reference: Lusk et al. 2025 (bioRxiv).

Prof. Dr. Thomas Seifert iLAND — Individual-based Forest Landscape and Disturbance Model
Also: Dr. Axel Albrecht · Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel · Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Jun.-Prof. Dr. Ana Stritih
Operational Process-based / Individual-based model
iLAND simulates growth, mortality, regeneration, and disturbance for every individual tree in a landscape at 100 m × 100 m resolution, from plot scale up to catchments of around 10 000 ha. Ecophysiological processes — carbon allocation, light competition, drought stress, wind, bark beetle, and fire — are represented explicitly. Silvicultural management is handled through the Agent-Based Engine (ABE) module, which links directly to participatory scenario work. iLAND is bidirectionally coupled with RoGeR.

Layer 2 — Biophysical Engine
Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler RoGeR — Runoff Generation Research Model (modular toolkit)
Operational Process-based hydrological model (no parameter calibration)
RoGeR models water redistribution at the surface and subsurface across three topographic settings — plateau (vertical flow only), hillslope (lateral subsurface inflow and outflow), and riparian zones (groundwater and capillary rise). All parameters are physically measurable; no calibration is required. The model operates from 1 m to several thousand km² and timesteps from one minute to one day. Within FuFo, it is being extended with a groundwater component through coupling to MODFLOW (Stahl) and has previously been coupled to bark beetle infestation risk at 10 m resolution (Weiler and Puhlmann).
Inputs
Climate forcing: precipitation (Prec), potential evapotranspiration (PET)NetCDF / CSV← Jung/Schindler, Christen (scenario-based forcing also from Stahl/C2)
Soil properties: texture, depth, hydraulic conductivity (directly measured — no calibration)GeoTIFF / CSV← Weiler (GIS)
Topography: slope, aspect, catchment delineationGeoTIFF← Weiler (GIS)
Canopy structure, leaf area index (LAI), root water uptake — bidirectional couplingNetCDF / CSV← iLAND
Outputs

Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel Forest Economics and Risk Model (GFPM-SW)
Also: Prof. Dr. Stefan Pauliuk
Planned Partial equilibrium / Economic optimisation
Develops an economic modelling and evaluation framework for ecosystem services under risk and uncertainty, applied to the outputs of the process-based growth models. The framework adapts the Global Forest Products Model (GFPM, Buongiorno et al.) for Southwest Germany as a partial equilibrium model implemented in GAMS. It takes ecosystem service outputs from iLAND and RoGeR, models their flow through demand, supply, industry, and networks of markets, and produces optimised robust management strategies using Value at Risk and Conditional Value at Risk metrics. Connected to the wood cascade model of Pauliuk to link into the industry sphere. Contributes preliminary Adaptation and Transformation Pathways for forests in SW Germany, including economic boundaries and robust management strategies.

Prof. Dr. Stefan Pauliuk Wood Cascade and Carbon Accounting (A&TPs)
Also: Dr. Simon Schulte
In Development Material flow analysis / Life cycle assessment
Develops a modelling and stakeholder engagement framework for identifying and evaluating Adaptation and Transformation Pathways for wood use and cascading. An A&TP is defined here as a sequence of decisions by agents in the wood use system — together with the resulting trajectory of system variables — that meets given objectives and respects given principles of responsibility and legitimacy. The work proceeds in three steps: (1) mapping the option space through scenarios for wood supply and demand; (2) combining wood processing data, stakeholder input on development goals, and model-based decision sequences to identify candidate pathways; (3) assessing the achievability of different goals under uncertainty and evaluating pathways for synergies, trade-offs, and robustness. A co-creation process with RA B and the SES-CaseLab is required for steps 2 and 3.

Prof. Dr. Heiner Schanz FESMarkets — Forest Ecosystem Service Markets
Planned System dynamics / Network of Markets
Analyses the structural architecture of Forest Ecosystem Service networks — covering water, recreation, protection, and timber — with a focus on how actual supply and demand for these services occurs through real markets (commodities, contracts) and political markets (subsidies), linked across complex value chain networks. The goal is twofold: to characterise the architecture of these networks, and to explore their governance dynamics as a basis for regulatory simulations and system dynamics modelling. A system dynamics model captures stocks and flows at the macro level; Causal Loop Diagrams represent actor perspectives and governance interventions at the micro level.

Dr. Calum Brown CRAFTY-EU — European Land Use Agent-Based Model
Also: Jun.-Prof. Dr. Ana Stritih · Prof. Dr. Maja Schlüter · Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel · Prof. Dr. Stefan Pauliuk
Planned Agent-based model / Land use model
CRAFTY-EU is an agent-based model operating at EU27+3 scale and 1 km resolution, embedded within the LandSyMM global framework that couples land use decision-making with macroeconomics, trade, ecosystem processes, biodiversity, and the climate system. Within FuFo, it provides the continental-scale context for the C3 models, upscaling local management patterns and ecosystem service outcomes and linking them to global scenarios (SSPs, RCPs, NFF). A key open challenge is bridging the scale difference between CRAFTY-EU (1 km) and iLAND (100 m).
