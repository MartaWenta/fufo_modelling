In Development Conceptual / Theoretical SES framework (not a computational model)
Key Question
What is a future forest as a social-ecological system — how do we define it, measure it, and talk about it across disciplines?
The theoretical and conceptual backbone of the entire Future Forests cluster. C1 develops a shared interdisciplinary social-ecological systems (SES) framework — including consistent definitions, indicators, accounting frameworks, and system boundaries — that all other research areas build on. It bridges natural science and social science vocabularies so that biophysical models and social models can be meaningfully connected. C1's framework provides the conceptual foundation for building comprehensive models and investigating Adaptation and Transformation Pathways (A&TPs) for novel forest SESs, as well as preparing, applying, and scaling governance innovations in C4, to provide a comprehensive and consistent depiction of future forests as novel SESs and their governance and management.

In Development Scenario development / Statistical downscaling / Stakeholder co-design
Key Question
What hydro-climatic and socio-economic conditions and extreme events will future forest social-ecological systems in our study region face — and what scenarios should guide planning and management?
C2 develops consistent and stakeholder-verified scenarios depicting the different future hydro-climatic and socio-economic conditions under which future forests will develop. These scenarios are a crucial input to modelling Adaptation and Transformation Pathways (A&TPs) in C3 and governance experimentation in C4. The consortium builds on multi-disciplinary expertise with studies using climate scenarios and regionally tailored downscaled climate projections (CMIP, CORDEX), bias corrections and weather generators for a range of applications including forest growth and economics. On the biophysical side, downscaled climate models focus on ensembles with relevant extreme events and disturbance triggers such as droughts, combining long-term climate model changes with event-storyline formulations. On the socio-economic side, C2 works with partners and stakeholders to co-create quantitative scenarios for the adaptation and transformation of socio-metabolic systems, drawing on existing national scenarios for timber supply. The scenarios developed in C2 specify local conditions in established global frameworks (SSPs, RCPs, Nature's Futures Framework) for the FuFo study regions.

Part of C2. Prof. Dr. Andreas Christen Adaptive Sensor Network & In-canopy Microclimate Model (A1)
Also: Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Dr. Florian Schnabel
In Development Machine learning / Spatial-temporal downscaling
Key Question
What are the exact temperature and humidity conditions inside forest canopies across the region — and how do they differ from the climate above the canopy?
Theme A1 investigates how climatic changes amplify or moderate within-forest microclimatic conditions and what drives this propagation. The work is built around approximately 300 observational forest plots and 40–50 research catchments across the study regions. WP1 develops a data-driven approach to model within-forest hydro-climatic conditions using an adaptive sensor network and machine learning. The sensor network records conditions at 300 plots within 100–150 stands and is self-optimising: after year 1, sites delivering redundant information are progressively abandoned and equipment moved to under-sampled conditions. The machine learning component follows three steps: (1) quality control and station uniqueness assessment; (2) hindcasting of in-canopy microclimates over 30 years at each sensor site; (3) a transferable regional model integrating geospatial predictors, sensor data and physical downscaling to predict below-canopy temperatures and humidity at any forested location in the FuFo domain.

Sensor network time series — air temperature and humidity from ~300 field plotsCSV / HDF5← FuFo adaptive sensor network (A1)
Geospatial predictors: forest structure, terrain (slope, aspect, elevation), soil propertiesGeoTIFF / LAS← Weiler (GIS) / Seifert
Outputs

Also: Dr. Axel Albrecht · Prof. Dr. Marc Hanewinkel · Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler · Jun.-Prof. Dr. Ana Stritih
Operational Process-based / Individual-based model
Key Question
How will forests grow, die, and change under future climates and different management decisions — and what timber, carbon, and ecosystem services will they provide?
The central simulation model of C3. Simulating how individual trees and entire forest landscapes grow, die, and respond to management and disturbance over time. iLAND tracks every individual tree in a landscape — its growth, mortality from drought, wind, bark beetle, or fire, and regeneration — at 100 m × 100 m resolution from plot scale to ~10 000 ha. It includes a management module (the Agent-Based Engine, ABE) that can represent different silvicultural strategies, directly linking to Stritih's participatory modelling work. iLAND is open-source, widely applied internationally, and bidirectionally coupled with the hydrological model RoGeR. Reference: Rammer et al. (2024), Ecological Modelling 495, 110785.

Layer 2 — Biophysical Engine
Prof. Dr. Markus Weiler RoGeR — Runoff Generation Research Model (modular toolkit)
Operational Process-based hydrological model (no parameter calibration)
Key Question
Which trees are most vulnerable to drought, and why — does it depend on the soil, the slope position, or access to groundwater?
Hydrological model toolkit that simulates how water moves through soils and landscapes, determining where trees have enough water and where they are at risk of drought stress. A key distinctive feature: no calibration of model parameters — all inputs are physically measurable soil and terrain properties, making the model directly interpretable. RoGeR is a modular family: RoGeR (core runoff processes), RoGeR_WB (full water balance, variable timestep), RoGeR_dyn (2D dynamic surface runoff), RoGeR_WB_Dyn (combined). Scale: 1 m to several 1000 km², timestep 1 min to 1 day. Three topographic contexts are modelled explicitly: plateau (1D, no lateral flow, current dynamic vegetation models only cover this case), hillslope (lateral subsurface inflow and outflow from upslope), and riparian/valley bottom (groundwater and capillary rise). This explicit representation of lateral subsurface flow explains tree-to-tree drought mortality differences on the same hillslope — a process missing from most dynamic vegetation models. In FuFo, Stahl is developing a coupling of RoGeR with the groundwater model MODFLOW, extending the system to deeper water cycle dynamics under climate stress (building on Hellwig et al. 2021). Weiler and Puhlmann have also coupled RoGeR to predict bark beetle infestation sites at 10 m resolution from soil water stress.

Climate forcing: precipitation (Prec), potential evapotranspiration (PET)NetCDF / CSV← Jung/Schindler, Christen (scenario-based forcing also from Stahl/C2)

Topography: slope, aspect, catchment delineationGeoTIFF← Weiler (GIS)

Which forest management strategies remain economically viable under climate uncertainty — and how will timber markets and wood prices respond?
